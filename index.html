About Me

Who Am I?

I'm a 4th year graduate student in the MIT-WHOI joint program. I'm working with Professor Henrik Schmidt in the Laboratory for Autonomous Marine Sensing Systems (LAMSS) . My research focuses on using signal processing and machine learning techniques to extract useful information from Arctic Ocean underwater ambient noise; these insights are then used to characterize the propagation environment and better inform underwater communication and navigation of autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs). In the past, I have worked on various other projects. I spent a summer working at the National Tsunami Warning Center in Hawai'i, developing a current velocity threshold for issuing warnings. This experience was my first ocean-related project and sparked my current interest into the field of ocean acoustics. As an undergraduate at Northwestern University, I particularly enjoyed physics and Earth science. I completed my honors thesis on a study of unusually cold and dense interstellar clouds and have also conducted research in isotope geochemistry prior to that. Indeed, my interests are varied and continuously evolving. I really enjoy the challenge and excitement that comes with breaking into an unfamilar field. Each day, I'm learning something new!

Outside of research, I love to cook and travel. I am an avid Northwestern Wildcats fan and enjoy following MIT athletics as well. I played the clarinet throughout high school and college and was proud to be a member of the Northwestern University "Wildcat" Marching Band. I now continue performing in the MIT concert Band and serve as the organization's webmaster.

My Research

Here are some of my current projects

Arctic Ocean Ambient Noise. Explores the feasibility of extracting useful environmental information from Arctic Ocean ambient noise to better inform underwater communication and navigation strategies in the region. One focus of this project involves the comparison of ambient noise data collected in the Beaufort Sea more than twenty years apart. Through spectral, spatial, and temporal analysis of the noise data, I was able to infer changes in ice cover properties that are a result of the Arctic's rapidly changing climate. The comparison also highlights a change in the spatial distribution of ambient noise generation in the ice cover, from a traditionally assumed uniform distribution to one that is more discrete and localized. More recently, I have been focusing on applying machine learning (ML) techniques such as convolutional neural networks (CNN) to tackle source localization and distribution characterization problems in underwater acoustics. Compared to conventional matched field processing (MFP) algorithms, ML approaches are showing more robustness to environmental mismatch when trained with an adequate amount of data.

Arctic Ocean Sound Speed Profile in 1994 and 2016

One major change in the Arctic propagation environment has been the emergence of the Beaufort Lens, a layer of warm water intruding in from the Pacific and neuturally buoyant around 80m depth. The change in the sound speed profile as a result has drastically altered sound propagation in the region.

CNN approach to source-range estimation compared to MFP

CNN technique shows more robustness to deviations to the Beaufort Lens strength as compared with MFP.

Vertical directionality of underwater ambient noise

Comparison of ambient noise vertical directionality for data collected in 1994 and 2016. There is a significant difference in the amount of arrival near 0 degrees elevation. Observed differences with modeling outputs for both times further questions the validity of our traditional assumptions.

A more discrete surface source distribution

Modeling output using a discrete surface source matches the observed 2016 results much more closely than the traditional assumed uniform surface source distribution.

Arctic Ice Cover Monitoring. This project focuses on the detection and localization of cryo-seismic events in the Arctic ice cover with a planar geophone array. I have been developing automatic event detection algorithms using unsupervised clustering techniques. The detected events are then localized via time-difference of arrival as well as ML methods. The results from this study help us better understand ice cover activity and the distribution of noise production in the ice.

cryo-seismic event localization

Event localization estimation using time-difference of arrival.

Adoption of CNN for event localization

Simulated events in the ice cover and their predicted locations from a CNN demonstrate the feasibility of ML techiques in tackling this type of problem.
